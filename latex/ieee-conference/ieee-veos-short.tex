\begin{abstract}
We present Energy Optimization System of Electric Vehicle (EOSEV), a deep-reinforcement-learning system for improving battery electric vehicle energy efficiency under real driving conditions. EOSEV formulates powertrain optimization as a Markov decision process over directly measurable signals: vehicle speed, accelerator and brake pedal openings, battery voltage, and battery current. The agent uses measured energy consumption as the reward and changes the vehicle control unit pedal map through bounded incremental torque updates, preserving the driver-in-the-loop production architecture. We design a dataflow for time-series capture, synchronization, replay-buffer sampling, online training, inference, cloud or on-board deployment, and over-the-air model updates. Real-vehicle experiments on a proving ground and public roads show continuous energy-consumption reduction, implicit exploitation of regenerative braking, and a weekly block-level reduction of about $7\%\sim8\%$ against baseline driving. We also analyze driving-style drift, transferability to another route, multimodal road scenarios, partial observability, sample efficiency, and deployment constraints.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
deep reinforcement learning, electric vehicle, time series, dataflow.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}

Drivers can consume different amounts of electricity on the same vehicle and route because longitudinal operation, especially acceleration and braking, strongly affects energy demand. This suggests a learning agent that observes driving dynamics, adjusts the powertrain response, and improves energy efficiency from easily available fleet data.

Applying deep reinforcement learning (DRL) in the real world remains difficult because of sample inefficiency, reward-shaping fragility, local optima, rare-pattern overfitting, unstable training, and hyperparameter sensitivity \parencite{DulacArnold_2021_real_world_rl}. Unlike games \parencite{mnih13:_playin_atari_deep_reinf_learn,DBLP:journals/nature/SilverHMGSDSAPL16,DBLP:conf/ijcai/BrownS17,openai19:_dota_large_scale_deep_reinf_learn,bakhtin22:_Human_level_diplomacy_cicero}, electric vehicles operate in noisy, partially observed, and non-repeatable environments. The practical path is therefore not a generic end-to-end recipe, but a combination of DRL with domain-specific state, action, reward, and deployment constraints.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:related}

EV DRL studies often choose model-specific states, actions, and shaped rewards \parencite{Egan_2023,Wang_2020,Hou_2022,Hu_2018}. Hybrid-vehicle energy management usually optimizes an engine/motor split with weighted fuel and electricity terms, while BEV eco-driving learns speed profiles, intersection behavior, and regenerative braking under simulated or connected-traffic assumptions \parencite{yeom2022mpc_drl_bev_ecodriving,li2023drl_ecodriving_traffic_uncertainties,zhang2022adaptive_ev_ecodriving}. Multi-speed BEV work uses SAC or DDPG for gear and torque operating points \parencite{yang2025drl_multispeed_pev_ems}. Across these lines, common gaps remain: limited real-vehicle validation, weak generalization across routes and weather, fragile reward weights, soft rather than hard constraint handling, simplified battery aging, inconsistent baselines, and under-addressed real-time deployment.

EOSEV differs by using measured energy consumption as the reward, directly available vehicle and driver signals as observations, and a bounded torque increment on the pedal map as the action. The goal is not to optimize a specific hybrid power split or autonomous speed plan, but to adapt production-relevant powertrain behavior conditioned on driver operation and road environment. The contributions are: a real-world MDP using measurable signals; an agent that modifies the VCU pedal map while preserving the existing control architecture; a practical dataflow for online training, inference, replay-buffer sampling, cloud/on-board deployment, and OTA updates; real-vehicle validation showing continuous reduction, implicit exploitation of regenerative braking \parencite{Yang_2024_RBS}, and baseline improvement; and analysis of stationarity, drift, transferability, multimodality, partial observability, sample efficiency, and deployment constraints.

The agent observes vehicle speed $V$, accelerator pedal opening $A$, and brake pedal opening $B$ from on-board sensors; $(V,A,B)$ is the EOSEV state. We focus on longitudinal control because accelerator and brake behavior dominate short-horizon energy variation. For example, at $50\,\mathrm{km/h}$ a representative $2500\,\mathrm{kg}$ EV needs about $3.4\,\mathrm{kW}$ for rolling resistance and $1.0\,\mathrm{kW}$ for aerodynamic drag on a flat road, while $0.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$ acceleration requires about $17.4\,\mathrm{kW}$ before drivetrain losses. Battery temperature, SOC, motor efficiency, and inverter efficiency influence absolute consumption and constraints, but are slow-varying, exogenous at the current decision step, or reflected in the measured voltage-current reward.

The instantaneous reward is the negative measured electric work, $-U I T_s$, where $U$ and $I$ are battery voltage and current and $T_s$ is the sample time. The action $\Delta\tau$ is an incremental torque-request change added to the vehicle control unit (VCU) pedal map.

The pedal map converts pedal opening and vehicle speed into electric-motor torque request. We use the calibrated ``Economic'' map as the initial policy and let the agent integrate bounded $\Delta\tau$ updates into the corresponding map region. Because the map includes negative torque at low speed and small pedal opening, the action space can implicitly exploit regenerative braking. Safety is preserved because the learned update does not bypass the production VCU: calibrated torque, voltage, current, thermal, diagnostic, and drivability limits remain the final authority, and candidate map updates are range-checked before deployment.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

Given the state, action, and reward above, we use a customized Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient algorithm \parencite{lillicrap15:_contin} based on \parencite{keras20:DDPG}. During training, the actor and critic are updated from replay-buffer samples; during inference, only the actor is required. The reward function $\mathcal{R}_a^s$ and transition dynamics $\mathcal{P}_{ss'}^{a}$ are provided by the real vehicle and driver.

\subsection{System Design and Dataflow}\label{system}

EOSEV supports training and inference locally over CAN/Ethernet or in the cloud through OTA updates, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:EOSEV_system}. A Scheduler configures dataflow states over CAN/UDP, the Cruncher receives observations from the ECU or cloud, forwards them to the Agent, writes records to Storage, and sends updated pedal maps back to the ECU. The small DDPG actor exports as a sub-1MB TFLite model, so inference is suitable for an ECU companion processor, TBox, or low-power system-on-chip. Production deployment should use timestamped observations, bounded queues, watchdog fallback to the nominal pedal map, and stale-action rejection; cloud training remains useful for fleet-scale updates, while on-board inference is preferred under strict latency budgets.

\subsection{Training}\label{sec:training}

During an episode, the system performs inference while collecting states, actions, and rewards. Afterward, the actor and critic are updated from replay-buffer batches. With fixed vehicle, route, and driver, we treat vehicle-road interaction and driving style as approximately stationary over short windows, then monitor style drift as the agent changes powertrain behavior.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

%If we regard the pedal map as the policy, then the the action we choose is actually is the score function of the policy.

Because efficient simulation of complete electric-powertrain behavior and complex road conditions is unavailable, EOSEV must learn from real data rather than rely on sim-to-real transfer. The experiments nevertheless show sample efficiency: hundreds of episodes are enough to produce a clear trend, mainly because the state, action, reward, and data processing avoid unnecessary noise. The transferability result also shows that training must be episodic to obtain meaningful reward signals, but inference with a frozen model can be non-episodic. Unlike many robotics tasks \parencite{DBLP:journals_ijrr_IbarzTFKPL21}, failed energy improvement does not interrupt the task, and VCU safeguards preserve physical limits during exploration.

\subsection{Future Work}\label{sec:future_work}

Future work should relax the stationary-driving-style assumption by treating the driver as part of a non-stationary or partially observable environment and adapting to distribution shift with recurrent policies, online adaptation, offline reinforcement learning, or driver-behavior models. The current DDPG policy observes only a few seconds, which is insufficient for ramps or longer topographic changes; recurrent deterministic policy gradient with LSTM and truncated BPTT \parencite{heess15:_memor,Hochreiter_1997,sutskever2013training,chollet2015keras} is a natural extension but requires more data and memory. Low-density records also make fleet-scale offline RL feasible: uploaded data can train IDQL-style policies \parencite{hansen-estruch23:_idql}, dispatch updates by OTA, and support asynchronous or federated training \parencite{mnih16:_async_method_deep_reinf_learn,konečný15:_feder_optim}. Diffusion policies may further model multimodal behavior \parencite{janner22:_plann_diffus_flexib_behav_synth,wang22:_diffus_polic_expres_polic_class,psenka23:_learn_diffus_model_polic_rewar}, but their denoising steps constrain real-time deployment.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We present EOSEV, a real-vehicle DRL system for improving EV energy efficiency by learning bounded pedal-map updates from directly measurable signals and measured energy consumption. The experiments show that careful state, action, reward, and dataflow design can make real-world reward-driven learning sample efficient, preserve the production driver-in-the-loop architecture, exploit regenerative braking implicitly, and reduce public-road energy consumption by about $7\%\sim8\%$ at weekly block level. The work also identifies practical requirements for scaling: monitoring driving-style drift, handling multimodal road scenarios, supporting frozen-model transfer, and using fleet data for recurrent or offline reinforcement learning.
