\begin{abstract}
	We present the application of deep reinforcement learning (DRL) in the optimization of energy efficiency of driving an electric vehicle. The optimization is modeled as Markov decision process (MDP) and the design choice of the state, action and reward is provided. We set up a flexible data pipeline for capturing, processing, storing and sampling time sequences which enables both online and offline reinforcement learning. The training and inference setup of the agent are provided with respect to the practical technicalities. Our system is scalable by leveraging the cloud infrastructure therefore is capable of handling a massive fleet in scope of the application asynchronously in training and inference deployment. We observe the manifest increase of the energy efficiency on the real road condition within a short time range of online training. We assume the driver behavior is stationary and monitor the driving style during training and give an analysis of the interaction between the human driver and the agent. We verify the transferability and multimodality of the trained model on different road conditions and give a review of safety, sample efficiency, data synchronicity of the system concerning the applications of deep reinforcement learning in the real world. Solutions are provided for solving partial observability, long-term strategy and efficient training of ragged episode length by leveraging sequential models. Deployment of reward-driven learning methods can promote the industry to leverage abundant available online or offline domain data and interfaces to achieve continuous and dynamic optimization in many complex industrial processes which require the large capacity of deep neural networks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Drivers with diverse driving experience tend to have quite different fuel or electricity consumption on the same vehicle and the same driving route. The general common sense is that the driving styles, i.e., how drivers operate the vehicle through acceleration and brake, have an impact on the vehicle energy consumption. We would expect there exists an experienced driver with a specific driving style can handle various road conditions to achieve the optimal energy efficiency. This leads us to the assumption that if we could apply an agent which observes the driving dynamics and adjusts the driving operation, we could reduce the energy consumption. If we choose the right observation and action, we could optimize the energy consumption online or offline for driving the vehicle with a general paradigm based on learning methods so that we can leverage a large amount of easily available driving data.

Nevertheless, the application of deep reinforcement learning in the real world is generally difficult \parencite{DulacArnold_2021_real_world_rl}. The main reasons include the sample inefficiency, reward shaping challenge, local optima, overfitting to rare patterns, unstable training, hyperparameter sensitivity. Most known successful applications of deep reinforcement learning can be found in games \parencite{mnih13:_playin_atari_deep_reinf_learn}, \parencite{DBLP:journals/nature/SilverHMGSDSAPL16}, \parencite{DBLP:conf/ijcai/BrownS17}, \parencite{openai19:_dota_large_scale_deep_reinf_learn}, \parencite{bakhtin22:_Human_level_diplomacy_cicero}, where the system dynamics are deterministic or closely deterministic.

In general, there's no generic end-to-end deep reinforcement learning method for real world applications, but a paradigm to combine deep reinforcement learning with domain specific techniques to alleviate the aforementioned challenges. As each problem in the real world has its specific prior information and exploitable inductive biases, it's only natural to exploit them in an engineering way.

This paper gives an example that progress in real world applications can be achieved with deep reinforcement learning. While research concerns might be alleviated with design choices, the practical deployment should still comply with the basic requirements of the theories stringently without loss of validity. Eventually in long term, issues of multimodality and out-of-distribution matter in complex and very long time horizon. On the practical side, deployment of reward-driven learning methods can promote the industry to leverage abundant available online or offline domain data and interfaces to achieve continuous and dynamic optimization in many complex industrial processes which require the large capacity of deep neural networks and have the potential to help reshaping the industry into the data-driven paradigm.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:related}

The applications of deep reinforcement learning in electric vehicles (EVs), for example in \parencite{Egan_2023}, \parencite{Wang_2020}, \parencite{Hou_2022}, \parencite{Hu_2018}, exploit the domain specific knowledge for each of the vehicle model under investigation to select the state and action. The constructed and estimated expectation state signals which are not directly measured contain further biases and noise. Besides, the measurable state signals in those applications require specific sensors or processing and are thus expensive to acquire. Furthermore, it should be noted that even the state of charge (SOC) cannot be directly measured in real time while the vehicle is driving so it's estimated from the measured voltage and a lookup table acquired by calibration. The selected actions serve their specific objective domains and therefore cannot be generalized to other vehicle models. The reward shaping in those applications are never the energy consumption directly, since the objective is to optimize the co-operation of electric motor and internal combustion engine (ICE) for hybrid electric vehicle (HEV)\@. It's usually a heuristic mixture of electricity and fuel consumption with constraints which are required to regularize the system behavior but in general detrimental to the optimization performance. The weights in the reward brings extra hyperparameters which are sensitive to the system performance and difficult to analyze.


Eco-driving is the dominant pure battery electric vehicle (BEV) DRL topic. The BEV energy problem is attractive for DRL because the agent can learn acceleration, cruising, regenerative braking, and intersection approach behavior under nonlinear constraints. \parencite{yeom2022mpc_drl_bev_ecodriving} combines model predictive control (MPC) and DRL for energy-efficient eco-driving, modeling BEV longitudinal dynamics, road slope, battery SOC, and traction power. The DRL component learns speed-control decisions, while MPC helps enforce trajectory feasibility and constraints. The paper reports energy/SOC savings across simulated driving scenarios compared with baseline driving policies. Another important line addresses connected BEVs at signalized intersections. \parencite{li2023drl_ecodriving_traffic_uncertainties} propose a hierarchical method: traffic uncertainty and queue effects are estimated first, then a DRL controller optimizes velocity under signal and safety constraints. This is significant because many earlier eco-driving methods assume perfect signal phase and timing (SPaT) and traffic information, which overstates savings. Earlier reinforcement-learning-based adaptive eco-driving for EVs along signalized corridors also frames the problem as real-time control under signal constraints, emphasizing adaptability over offline optimal control \parencite{zhang2022adaptive_ev_ecodriving}. DRL consistently shows potential to reduce BEV energy use by smoothing speed profiles and avoiding inefficient braking/acceleration. However, most papers rely on simulation, simplified driver/traffic models, and limited battery-aging treatment.

Pure BEVs historically used single-speed drivetrains, but multi-speed BEVs create a genuine onboard energy management system (EMS) problem: selecting gear and torque operating points to reduce motor/inverter/battery losses. \parencite{yang2025drl_multispeed_pev_ems} studies multi-speed pure electric vehicles using soft actor-critic (SAC) and deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) to minimize energy loss. They avoid the fuel-engine split problem of HEV literature and focus on BEV powertrain efficiency. This stream is smaller than eco-driving but important for future BEVs with two-speed or multi-speed transmissions, commercial BEVs, and performance-oriented EVs. However, attributes of the chosen state (speed and acceleration) are chronologically correlated and contain complex system dynamics which might reduce the efficiency of policy learning, while the transmission gear as the action doesn't have a direct impact on the torque exertion. 

The main weaknesses are quite consistent across the field:
\begin{itemize}
\item Simulation dominance: Few studies validate on real BEVs, hardware-in-the-loop platforms, or production ECUs.
\item Weak generalization: Policies trained on one drive cycle, road grade, traffic condition, or ambient temperature often degrade elsewhere.
\item Reward engineering fragility: Small reward-weight changes can shift behavior toward unsafe, uncomfortable, or battery-damaging strategies.
\item Constraint handling: Safety, traffic rules, battery voltage/current/temperature limits, and comfort are often penalties rather than hard guarantees.
\item Battery aging simplification: Many studies use SOC/temperature proxies instead of validated degradation models.
\item Benchmark inconsistency, Baselines vary widely: rule-based control, dynamic programming (DP), MPC, human driving, or simplified optimal policies, making results hard to compare.
\item Real-time deployment gaps: Computational latency, sensor uncertainty, vehicle-to-everything (V2X) loss, and certification are under-addressed.
\end{itemize}

Compared to these methods, we can use the energy consumption as reward, while taking observation directly from the vehicle dynamics and driver behavior. The objective would not be the optimization of the co-operation of the mixed powertrain components, but the optimization of overall powertrain dynamic behavior conditioned on the road environment and driver operation. We'd call such a system ``Energy Optimization System of Electric Vehicle'' (EOSEV). Such a system would be generic and applicable for any type of EVs since all the required observations, actions and rewards would be available and easily measurable. The challenge is to learn the complex system dynamics of driving in the real world condition.

We observe in the experiment that even without explicitly modeling the regenerative braking system (RBS) \parencite{Yang_2024_RBS} behavior in the system, it is actively and legitimately exploited by the agent to reduce the energy consumption without any explicit built-in rules or modeling based on the observations of the road conditions or driver operations. 

The specific contributions of this work are as follows. First, we formulate the energy optimization of a pure electric vehicle as a real-world MDP using directly measurable vehicle signals, namely vehicle speed, accelerator pedal opening, brake pedal opening, battery voltage, and battery current, so that the reward is the measured energy consumption rather than a heuristic surrogate. Second, we introduce EOSEV, a deep-reinforcement-learning-based energy optimization system that modifies the powertrain pedal map through an incremental torque action while preserving the existing vehicle control architecture. Third, we design and implement a practical dataflow that supports online training, inference, replay-buffer sampling, cloud or on-board deployment, and over-the-air (OTA) model updates for scalable fleet-level operation. Fourth, we validate the method with real vehicle experiments on a proving ground and public roads, showing continuous energy-consumption reduction, implicit exploitation of regenerative braking, and measurable improvement over baseline driving. Finally, we analyze deployment-relevant properties including driving-style stationarity and drift, transferability of a frozen model to a different route, multimodal road scenarios, partial observability, sample efficiency, and the feasibility of extending the system with recurrent and offline reinforcement learning methods.

When the agent is connected to the regular system as depicted, it will get the vehicle speed $V$, the driver's operation on the acceleration pedal $A$ and the brake pedal $B$ from the on-board sensors as its observation. $(V,A,B)$ is defined as the state of EOSEV system. In order to reduce the observation dimensionality, we take only the longitudinal control the vehicle, namely the electric powertrain into account, since the lateral operation through the steering wheel has far less impact on the energy consumption than the longitudinal control.

This design choice is also supported by a simple power-scale comparison. The longitudinal road-load power can be approximated as $P_{\mathrm{long}}=v(m a_x+mgC_{rr}+\frac{1}{2}\rho C_d A_f v^2+mg\sin\theta)$, where $a_x$ is longitudinal acceleration, $C_{rr}$ is the tire rolling-resistance coefficient, $\rho$ is air density, $C_d$ is the aerodynamic drag coefficient, and $A_f$ is frontal area. For a representative electric vehicle with $m=2500\,\mathrm{kg}$, $C_{rr}=0.01$, $C_dA_f=0.7\,\mathrm{m^2}$, and $v=50\,\mathrm{km/h}$ on a flat road, the rolling-resistance and aerodynamic terms are approximately $3.4\,\mathrm{kW}$ and $1.0\,\mathrm{kW}$, respectively. A moderate acceleration of $0.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$ already requires about $17.4\,\mathrm{kW}$ of inertial power, and $1.0\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$ requires about $34.7\,\mathrm{kW}$, before drivetrain losses are considered. Thus ordinary accelerator and brake behavior can change the instantaneous power demand by several times the steady road-load power, whereas steering affects energy mainly through secondary tire-scrub and path-length effects under normal low-speed operation. Therefore the first-order optimization variables are the longitudinal signals $V$, $A$, and $B$.

Other powertrain variables, such as battery temperature, state of charge (SOC), motor efficiency, and inverter efficiency, are not included in the state space for the same reason. They influence the absolute energy consumption and operating constraints, but they are either slow-varying over the short training episodes, exogenous to the driver-agent decision at the current time step, or already reflected in the measured voltage-current product used as the reward. EOSEV does not need to estimate these internal efficiency states explicitly in order to improve the pedal-map policy; it only needs a reliable observation of the driver's longitudinal demand and a dense physical reward that evaluates the resulting powertrain work. Excluding these variables therefore reduces observation dimensionality and sensor dependence without changing the effectiveness of the system in the considered experiment.

Given a series of $(V,A,B)$ for a short period of time, an experienced human driver is usually able to predict and choose the next appropriate strategy to accelerate or decelerate in an energy efficient way. The negative of the energy consumption, which is the product of the voltage $U$ and the current $I$ from the sensors in powertrain, $U\cdot I$, multiplied by the system sample time $T_s$, is taken as the instantaneous reward which is approximately the electric work the powertrain has done in that time step. Thus we have the energy consumption as the reward in the physical sense. The action $\Delta\tau$ is chosen to be the change of torque request incrementally added onto the ``pedal map'' of the vehicle control unit (VCU)\@.

The pedal map is a two-dimensional table which outputs the torque request to the electric motor for a given acceleration pedal opening and a certain vehicle speed. It's a static map for a regular powertrain controller, as depicted in Fig.\@\ref{fig:initial pedal map}. The requested torque, once exerted by the electric motor, is proportional to the vehicle acceleration. It's usually acquired by vehicle calibration during the pre-development of the EV model. A calibration engineer will generate different pedal maps by testing drives on proving ground or real road and determine table items empirically and by experimental validation. The different pedal maps will correspond to different driving styles which are provided as features to the customers, such as ``Normal'', ``Sport'' or ``Economic''. The customers will choose a pedal map through a vehicle human-machine interface (HMI) to match their driving style requirements. We select the ``Economic'' pedal map as the default initial pedal map for the agent and check how much the agent can further improve the energy efficiency. The pedal map increases monotonically with the acceleration pedal opening, while decreasing monotonically with the vehicle speed. The maximal torque request occurs when the vehicle starts off and the driver steps on the pedal with full throttle. The map region with low speed ($\leq10kmph$) and negligible pedal openings has the largest the negative torque request, which corresponds to motor in reverse, i.e.\ strong regenerative braking. These are all by design.

When the agent changes the torque request for a given range of vehicle speeds under a given pedal opening, the corresponding region of the pedal map will be changed by $\Delta\tau$. If the training occurs on an urban road, we can see the low speed region under 60 kmph is changed as depicted in Fig.\ref{fig:dynamic pedal map}. The resulting pedal map is the sum of the initial map and integration of the action up to the current moment. In our experiment the agent is allowed to change the items all over the map region, which allows implicitly the exploitation of the regenerative braking.

Dynamically modifying the pedal map is safe in this architecture because the learned action does not bypass the production vehicle control unit (VCU). The VCU remains the final actuator authority and enforces the calibrated torque, voltage, current, thermal, diagnostic, and drivability limits before any motor command is executed. In addition, the data pipeline validates each candidate pedal-map update before deployment: the updated table must remain within predefined safe ranges, preserve basic calibration constraints such as bounded torque requests, and pass consistency checks against invalid sensor values or abnormal operating states. The agent is also restricted to incremental modifications, so each update only changes the existing calibrated map by a bounded $\Delta\tau$ instead of replacing the controller behavior with an unconstrained policy. Therefore exploration is limited to a validated neighborhood of the production calibration, while the VCU safeguards continue to protect the physical powertrain.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

Once the design choice for the state, action, and reward are made, we apply the standard reinforcement learning, see Fig.\@\ref{fig:policy gradient}. We use a customized Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient algorithm \parencite{lillicrap15:_contin} due to its sample efficiency and continuous action space. The implementation is based on \parencite{keras20:DDPG}. During training, both the critic and the actor are updated with gradient descent by the reward signal, while for inference only the actor is required. Both the reward function $\mathcal{R}_a^s$ which generates the energy consumption and the system dynamic $\mathcal{P}_{ss'}^{a}$ which determines the state transition come from the real world. During training we keep a replay buffer to take new observations and sample batches from it.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

The training setup is important for the agent to learn a good policy. As a basic requirement for reinforcement learning, the training should be episodic. We first choose a short episode with a target speed profile as depicted in Fig.\@\ref{fig:speed profile for training}, while the vehicle is simply driven forward. From a standstill, the vehicle needs to be accelerated to a maximal speed of around 17kmph. After deceleration it returns to its standstill state. An upper bound and lower bound are set for allowed maximal deviation from the desired target speed. If the actual speed is out of the bound for longer than three seconds, the test episode is invalid or should be interrupted prematurely and the data is abandoned. The test is carried out on a proving ground with no traffic or pedestrian. With this simple speed profile we expect the agent to learn the basic policy of acceleration and deceleration and thus be prepared for more complex scenarios.

\subsection{System Design and Dataflow}\label{system}

The EOSEV system is a dual-pipeline framework that supports both training and inference for the deep reinforcement learning model, either locally on the on-board computing units via controller area network (CAN) or Ethernet, or in the cloud through OTA updates, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:EOSEV_system}. Because the pedal map is updated only when traffic conditions change, the control action $\Delta \tau$ does not require hard real-time guarantees. We therefore run training and inference in the cloud to preserve the current vehicle electrical/electronic (E/E) architecture and share the solution across the product line.

A Scheduler configures and schedules the dataflow states over CAN/user datagram protocol (UDP), either through a local Kvaser CAN server (KvaserCAN) or through a cloud object storage service endpoint (CloudOSS). The Cruncher acts as the data hub: it receives observations from the electronic control unit (ECU) or the cloud, forwards them to the Agent, and sends the updated pedal map back to the ECU. The Agent returns the updated pedal map, i.e., the action, to the Cruncher, which stores the observations and actions in Storage and provides observation batches to the Model for training and action generation.

For production deployment, the real-time processing latency is dominated by signal acquisition, message scheduling, and the update path to the ECU rather than by neural-network inference. The DDPG actor used for inference is a small MLP exported as a sub-1MB TFLite model, so it is suitable for standard automotive embedded hardware such as an ECU companion processor, TBox, or low-power system-on-chip without a dedicated accelerator. Because each record spans 3 seconds and the reward is assigned over the following 1.5 seconds, millisecond-scale inference and local preprocessing latency is well below the control interval. The remaining engineering requirement is therefore bounded end-to-end latency: timestamped observations, bounded queues, watchdog fallback to the nominal pedal map, and stale-action rejection should be used so that communication jitter cannot turn an old action into a current torque request. Cloud inference or cloud training remains useful for fleet-scale updates, but on-board inference is preferred whenever a strict real-time latency budget is required.

\subsection{Training}\label{sec:training}

When an episode starts, the system performs inference while collecting states, actions, and rewards until the episode ends. The actor and critic are then updated using batches sampled from the replay buffer.

As shown in Fig.\@\ref{fig:Agent_Powertrain}, the reinforcement learning environment includes both the vehicle on the road and the driver. When the vehicle and test route are fixed, we expect the vehicle-road interaction to be stationary. We also keep the driver fixed during testing, since each driver is expected to have a consistent driving style; therefore, we assume the driving style is stationary as well. Because driving style varies across drivers and can drift during interaction with the agent, we monitor it to analyze its impact on the overall EOSEV system.

\subsubsection{Driving style}\label{sec:driving style}

It should be noted that a consumption increase in week 2 is due to a temperature drop, which causes the battery management system to turn on battery heating and increase the overall consumption. This indicates that the EOSEV system is weather resistant. Using the reported weekly reductions in Tab.\@\ref{tab:openroad a result}, $8.16\%$, $7.56\%$, and $7.03\%$, the mean reduction is $7.58\%$ with a standard deviation of $0.57\%$. To test whether this reduction is statistically distinguishable from zero, we perform a one-sample $t$-test on the three weekly reductions. The result is $t(2)=23.23$ with a two-sided $p=0.0018$. The 95\% confidence interval for the mean reduction is $[6.18\%, 8.99\%]$. Therefore, the reported weekly reductions provide statistically significant evidence supporting the claim that the agent reduces energy consumption by approximately $7\%\sim8\%$. Because the test is based on three weekly aggregate values, the result should be interpreted as block-level evidence; a stronger analysis would use per-episode consumption data within each week.

Compared with published BEV eco-driving and energy-optimization studies, this result is in the competitive range for a real-road, human-driver experiment. Simulation-based connected and automated vehicle methods often report larger nominal savings: for example, a DRL and MPC eco-driving method for BEVs reports about $1\%$ saving on a simple uphill route and $17\%$ on routes with multiple ascents and descents in CarSim \parencite{yeom2022mpc_drl_bev_ecodriving}, while a PPO-based connected automated driving method reports $9.3\%$--$21.9\%$ simulated energy reduction depending on the baseline \parencite{min2023energy_efficient_driving_cav_rl}. Recent optimal-control work with driver-preference models reports $21\%$ saving over a simulated 25 km route, but with a $7\%$ decrease in average speed, and $10\%$--$15\%$ in car-following scenarios without average-speed loss \parencite{lot2025ecodriving_ev_driver_preferences}. The $7\%\sim8\%$ reduction reported here is therefore below the upper bound claimed by fully simulated or automated trajectory-optimization methods, but it is achieved without assuming full autonomous longitudinal planning, perfect preview information, or a simulated-only validation environment. This places EOSEV as a practically promising approach relative to the state of the art, especially because it optimizes the powertrain control parameters under real driving conditions while preserving the driver's role in the loop.

In Fig.\@\ref{fig:openroad a style}, the orange and the blue curve correspond to the KL divergence of the episodic driving style with and without the agent respectively. A drift in driving style while training can be seen over around 600 episodes. In the long run, the driving style will be impacted by the agent changing the powertrain behavior, if the driver is exploring a cooperative driving strategy. The agent is adaptive to this change.

We test for a long time so as to get sufficient sample episodes for each mode. In the analysis, we filter out the different modes for comparison. \section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

%If we regard the pedal map as the policy, then the the action we choose is actually is the score function of the policy.

Unlike in the games or robotics, we cannot leverage simulation to provide a large amount of training data and cannot do sim2real techniques to accelerate the learning, because there's no available efficient simulation for electric powertrain and complex road condition. Part of the reason is that modeling the real road condition and electric motor is very difficult or expensive, which is the normal case for most of the real world applications. We have to learn from the real world. Surprisingly, our experiment shows that the application of deep reinforcement learning is quite sample efficient in the energy optimization system. We only need hundreds of episodes to see a clear improvement trend in the result. We believe it's due to the careful design choice of state, action, reward and the data processing that avoids much noise in the state and reward.

The result of transferability in Sec.\@\ref{sec:transferable} with frozen model indicates that while the training must be episodic in order to have meaningful reward signals, the inference-only mode doesn't need to be episodic. A trained frozen model can be deployed for non-episodic situations in inference-only mode in a wide range of applications without the episodic constraint.

A practical advantage of the application is that system safety and task persistency are not issues like in the robotics \parencite{DBLP:journals_ijrr_IbarzTFKPL21}, since the optimization of improving energy efficiency is almost not safety relevant. The agent is free to explore diverse strategies with the safeguard of VCU. Failing to reduce the energy consumption will never interrupt the episode, thus the training is reliable, stable and efficient.

\subsection{Future Work}\label{sec:future_work}

The stationary driving-style assumption in the experiments is useful for experimental control and for attributing changes in energy consumption to the agent, but it is not a necessary condition for applying reinforcement learning. If the driver's behavior changes over time, the driver can be treated as part of the environment dynamics, and the resulting problem can still be approached as an online, non-stationary, or partially observable reinforcement learning problem. In that case, the agent must adapt to distribution shift in the observed state-action-reward sequences rather than rely on a fixed behavior distribution. A natural future direction is therefore to explicitly model and learn under driving-style drift, either through recurrent policies, online adaptation, offline reinforcement learning from fleet data, or behavior models that track the driver's changing policy during interaction with EOSEV.

In the experiment the DDPG policy is short-term with only several seconds of observation. The results match the experience that good human drivers usually don't need long-term strategy to make the right decision for acceleration or braking. However, long-term observations benefit the right decision. For ramps or different topographies of road surfaces which take more than 3 seconds to pass through, the DDPG agent with the current short attention span will not be able to have an optimal policy. To deal with this partial observability, a recurrent deterministic policy gradient (RDPG) agent \parencite{heess15:_memor} is implemented with the long short-term memory (LSTM) networks \parencite{Hochreiter_1997}. With truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT) \parencite{sutskever2013training} and the stateful feature of LSTM cell \parencite{chollet2015keras}, we can handle arbitrary ragged episode lengths and do inference efficiently with long and short term policies. Extensive road tests with sequential neural network model need yet to be done which would require expectedly much more data due to the increase of the model dimensionality.


The data in Tab.\@\ref{tab:quadruple} has a low density and is light-weight for collection and storage. In order to leverage the large volume of offline data with ongoing training, an offline reinforcement learning is implemented with implicit diffusion Q-learning (IDQL) \parencite{hansen-estruch23:_idql}. With the offline data, training can occur on the cloud with uploaded observation data from vehicles. The updated local model can be dispatched onto the vehicle through OTA communication. In this framework, the training and inference can be done flexibly either locally or on the cloud, see Fig.\@\ref{fig:Software overview}. Training and inference locally are fast, have better real-time performance and signal quality, but need extra compute resources on the vehicle. The DDPG agent has a size of less than 1MB as exported TensorFlow Lite (tflite) models which can be easily stored on the embedded system and used in inference, while it's still difficult to accommodate the RDPG agent with its nearly 100 MB on most of the current embedded system in production.\ On the cloud it's more scalable, doesn't need much compute locally but OTA communication with less real-time performance, larger signal latency and lower signal quality. In large scale deployment, training and inference on the cloud also provide an opportunity for utilization of parallel training of asynchronous policy gradient methods \parencite{mnih16:_async_method_deep_reinf_learn} or federated learning \parencite{konečný15:_feder_optim}. 

Diffusion models have been studied actively in recent offline reinforcement learning researches, in particular in behavior cloning for policy learning \parencite{janner22:_plann_diffus_flexib_behav_synth} \parencite{wang22:_diffus_polic_expres_polic_class} and \parencite{hansen-estruch23:_idql} \parencite{psenka23:_learn_diffus_model_polic_rewar}. Besides utilized by the offline reinforcement learning, the diffusion models can be used to recover complex and multimodal behavior data. With the diffusion model to fit the behavior policy, the drift of the driving style in Fig.\@\ref{fig:openroad a style} is expected to be better tracked by the implicit actor with policy extraction \parencite{hansen-estruch23:_idql}. However, the training and sampling in diffusion models require extra noising and denoising time steps, which reduce its real-time performance and constrains their application. All these improvements require more compute resources and need further verification with large scale deployment.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We present the application of deep reinforcement learning in the optimization of energy efficiency of driving an electric vehicle. We demonstrate that the design choice of the state, reward and action is crucial. If the reward is rich and dense, the application in real world should be sample efficient. If the observation data are of low density and generally accessible like vehicle speed, acceleration and braking pedal opening but contain complex behaviors or system models, we could harvest a large volume of offline data and leverage offline reinforcement learning to improve the optimization process. As reward-driven learning methods are machine learning methods which require no supervision or labeling, but learn from large amount of raw data, they are appealing to the industrial applications. Our experiment is still far from being scaled up. With deployment with a large number of vehicles in the future, we hope to track complex system dynamics, road scenarios and driving behaviors by more efficient reinforcement learning methods.

The purpose of our work is twofold. On the engineering side, we hope to advocate in the industry to leverage abundant available online or offline domain data and interfaces to achieve continuous and dynamic optimization in complex industrial processes which require the large capacity of deep neural networks and have the potential to help reshaping the industry into the data-driven paradigm.

For research, we intend to provide an application of deep reinforcement learning in the real world that provides interesting and challenging optimization goals. Unlike games or robotics, we cannot always leverage simulation in the real world, but we can have abundant data and achieve sample efficiency with prudent design choice of state, action and reward and by careful implementation which maintains the signal causality and avoids adding noise to state and reward signal. Furthermore, system and task persistency are sometimes guaranteed. More importantly, with large scale deployment of data-driven method and the improvement of the industrial process optimization, a virtuous cycle would contribute to providing abundant data, applying new research results and finding new interesting research challenges.
